# 02 — Company Bible: AUREL

**Owner:** Narrative Lead + Design Director
**Audience:** Every writer producing a scene set inside AUREL, every designer specifying a UI string in the AUREL voice, every developer naming a feature.
**Status:** Canon. Read together with `01_world_bible.md`. The world doc explains 2034. This doc explains AUREL inside 2034.
**Scope:** AUREL — origin, founder, HQ, London office, products, ladder, culture, rituals, aesthetic. **Not** the cast (`03_npc_bible_*.md`), **not** the gameplay (`08_annual_review.md`, `07_question_bank_*.md`).

---

## 1. How to use this document

AUREL is the building. Everything in ASCEND happens inside it, near it, or because of it. Writers do not need to mention AUREL in every scene; they need to be unable to write a scene that contradicts AUREL.

Three rules:

1. **AUREL is specific.** Where AUREL details exist in this doc, they are exclusive. The CEO is Elena Voss, not Eleanor Vance. The HQ is Chicago, not Austin. The flagship product is Halo. Writers who reach for plausible-sounding alternatives in scenes are introducing canon errors.
2. **Show the gap.** AUREL has stated values and actual rules. The gap is the comedy. Every NPC scene should let the gap breathe somewhere, even if only for a beat.
3. **The company is the antagonist and the protagonist.** AUREL gets the player promoted and AUREL crushes them. Both are true. The game does not tilt; writers should not tilt either. A scene that reads as "AUREL is just bad" or "AUREL is just good" is not yet finished.

## 2. Premise: what AUREL is

AUREL is a US-headquartered consumer-tech behemoth whose products function as social infrastructure for status-conscious adults. It does not make phones. It makes the things that quietly replace phones — wearables, ambient AI, spatial collaboration, and the identity-and-presence layer that ties them together.

AUREL's market position in 2034 is the closest thing to *Apple in 2012 crossed with Stripe in 2024 crossed with the IBM of the early 1960s.* Beautiful objects, indispensable infrastructure, slightly intimidating institutional gravity. A $900B market cap. Roughly 38,000 employees globally. Three product pillars. One ambient mood, which is **calm prestige.**

The phrase "calm prestige" is not marketing. It is the thing the company actually believes about itself. It is also how the company misreads itself when things go wrong.

## 3. Origin story

### 3.1 The founder

**Elena Voss.** Born 1992, raised in Boston, BA Economics from Brown, MBA from Wharton. Joined McKinsey out of business school, made partner unusually fast, left in 2025 at age 33 to start a company. The company she started was not AUREL.

The first company — **Aurelis Identity** — was an enterprise digital-identity firm. It was small, dull, profitable, and on track to be acquired by a consultancy in 2027 for an unremarkable price. Voss did not enjoy running it. By every account, she was bored.

In 2028, she made the move that defines the AUREL myth. She used Aurelis's cash position and steady contracts to acquire **Halcyon Form**, a near-bankrupt consumer hardware studio in Chicago that had been working on an ambient-wearable concept for three years and had never shipped. The acquisition was widely seen as a vanity move. The board nearly fired her over it.

The reasoning Voss gave at the time, in a memo that was later leaked and became part of the company mythology: *"Identity infrastructure with no human surface is a margin business with no future. Halcyon is a human surface with no infrastructure. Apart they are both dying. Together they are the next decade."*

She was right. The combined entity was renamed **AUREL** (a play on Aurelis and the Latin *aurum*, gold). The first Halo prototype shipped in late 2029. By 2031 it was the fastest-selling premium wearable in history.

### 3.2 What Voss is like (canon, for writers)

- **Public image:** precise, elegant, impossible to rush. Speaks in short sentences. Surgical compliments. No exclamation marks.
- **Private truth:** she came up through operational chaos and has no patience for decorative intelligence. Distrusts founders who perform the founder role. Fires people for being interesting in the wrong way.
- **Voice:** dry, observational, slightly amused. Uses "interesting" the way a surgeon uses "concerning."
- **Aesthetic:** bone-coloured wool, no jewelry, single watch worth more than your car. Walks fast. Reads everything.
- **Contradiction:** she despises political theater and is one of the best performers in the building.
- **Power signature:** she does not raise her voice. She becomes quieter. Three-second silences from Voss have ended careers.

Voss is **almost never on screen** in ASCEND. She is referenced. Her decisions reach the player through other people's faces. The single canonical Voss appearance is a Year 11 scene if the player reaches it. Until then, she is a weather system.

### 3.3 The founding decade in five dates

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 2025 | Voss leaves McKinsey, founds Aurelis Identity in Chicago. |
| 2028 | Aurelis acquires Halcyon Form. The combined entity becomes AUREL. The board nearly fires Voss. |
| 2029 | First Halo ships. NHS digital-identity contract signed. AUREL goes from boring to consequential in nine months. |
| 2030 | Loom (the AI orchestration layer) launches. Halo + Loom becomes the bundle. |
| 2031 | IPO at $310B. Voss keeps voting control. The post-IPO letter to shareholders contains one sentence: *"We will continue to make beautiful things that work."* |
| 2033 | Glassroom (spatial collaboration) launches. AUREL becomes a three-product company — by design. |

## 4. Headquarters: Chicago

### 4.1 Why Chicago (canon reasoning, in case any scene asks)

Voss chose Chicago for four reasons, in this order:

1. **Distance from coastal narrative.** A company headquartered in San Francisco would be measured against San Francisco. A company headquartered in Chicago would have to be measured on its own terms.
2. **Architectural seriousness.** Voss said in a 2030 interview that "buildings shape companies." She wanted AUREL inside a building that looked like it intended to outlast her.
3. **Operational discipline.** Chicago has a long industrial-and-commercial tradition. Voss wanted hires who would treat shipping a wearable like shipping anything else — a manufactured object with a deadline.
4. **Talent economics.** Chicago is cheaper than the Bay, deeper than Austin, and underused.

### 4.2 The campus

AUREL HQ occupies a 12-acre reclaimed warehouse-district site in the West Loop, designed by a Japanese-Norwegian architectural collective. From outside, it looks like a luxury logistics hub: concrete, glass, dark steel, a single line of mature trees along the north edge.

Inside:

- **No meeting rooms under twenty minutes.** This is famously a Voss rule. Quick conversations happen in standing pods or walking corridors. The campus is laid out to make ten-minute hallway conversations physically possible — long, slightly curved corridors with acoustic panels, lined with windows.
- **The Sensory Garden.** A 4,200-square-foot enclosed atrium where long, difficult conversations happen. Soft light, no recording, water feature audible at ~38 dB. Treated like a chapel.
- **The Wall.** A single 60-meter wall in the main lobby, displaying every shipped AUREL product since 2029 in chronological order. New employees are walked past it on day one. Departing employees, by tradition, walk past it on their last day. The Wall is not photographed. There are signs.
- **No open seating for executives.** SVP and above have their own offices. Everyone else hot-desks with anchor zones.
- **The kitchens.** AUREL operates twelve kitchens across the campus. The food is widely understood to be one of the great retention strategies in modern technology. There is a six-month waiting list for the head chef's pop-up table.
- **The basement.** Rumored to contain prototype labs nobody has seen photographs of. Probably true. Treated as canon: there are basement labs and most employees have never been inside one.

### 4.3 What it feels like to visit Chicago HQ (writer's reference)

Coastal employees who fly in for "Chicago weeks" describe the experience in a recurring way: *"It's calmer than I expected, and that calm is itself slightly threatening."* Meetings start exactly on time. Disagreements happen at lower volume. The phrase "interesting" gets used the way Voss uses it. The food is genuinely excellent. The hotel AUREL puts you in is beautiful and slightly impersonal. By the third day, most visitors have either fallen in love with Chicago HQ or developed a low hum of unease they cannot articulate. Both reactions are common; neither is correct.

When a London player visits Chicago in a later year (V2.0+), this is the texture to evoke.

## 5. London office: King's Cross / N1C

### 5.1 The building

AUREL London occupies floors 41–48 of a restored mid-century industrial building on Stable Street, Coal Drops Yard. The exterior is brick and dark steel; the interior is bone-white walls, oak floors, and the kind of acoustically engineered silence that costs a great deal of money to achieve.

The building has **two private lifts** — one for floors 41–44 (most of the office) and one for floors 45–48 (executive, partnerships, regulatory affairs). Mixing lifts is technically allowed and socially noticed.

The reception is a single curved oak desk staffed by **Grace Oduya** (see NPC Bible) during the day and a quiet rotation of contractors at night. There is no security theater. There is also no way to enter without ThreadWallet authorization.

The view from floor 47 (regulatory) is the best view in the building. Voss has stayed in floor 47's corner office once, for ninety minutes, in 2032. The chair has not been moved since.

### 5.2 The London setup

London is **officially** a satellite. London is **functionally** the EMEA power center. The unwritten rule is that London handles:

- **EU regulatory translation.** Everything that needs to pass the AI Act, the Digital Services Act, and the various national-level wearable rules gets routed through London.
- **Premium retail and partnerships.** Halo's luxury-retail relationships in Europe (LVMH, Compass, certain hotel groups) are owned by London.
- **Enterprise pilots.** NHS, the European Commission, and major UK banks — London-led.
- **Future-market experimentation.** Unannounced product trials happen in London because Chicago has decided London is "small enough to fail in." Londoners know this. They use it.

The London-Chicago dynamic that writers need to internalize: **Chicago thinks London is decorative. London knows it isn't.** Senior London people have built careers on being indispensable in a way Chicago can never quite fully credit. The relationship is functional, the resentment is real, and the comedy lives in the gap.

### 5.3 Office culture

The London office has its own micro-culture, distinct from Chicago.

- **Multilingual.** A typical floor-44 lunch table has four languages running. Code-switching is constant.
- **Politically literate.** People dress and speak as if a product launch and a nervous breakdown might happen the same afternoon. Both regularly do.
- **Crisp.** No founder cosplay, no hoodies, no fake-casual. London tech in 2034 dresses one notch up from American tech, and AUREL London dresses one notch up from London tech.
- **Quiet.** The office is genuinely quiet. Halo handles most ambient notification. Voices are low.
- **Coffee snobbery.** The 4th-floor café has a single-origin pour-over rotation. There is a Slack channel called `#bean-discourse` that nobody admits to following.
- **The Friday Pulse.** Every Friday at 16:00, the EMEA Director sends a narrative-stream summary of the week to the whole office. Reading it is mandatory. Replying is not. Replying smartly is high-status.

### 5.4 Why the player starts in London (canon reasoning)

The player joins AUREL London for one of three reasons, depending on background:

- **Local hire.** UK-based graduate; London is the natural placement.
- **EMEA assignment.** International student or non-UK graduate placed in London because London is where new graduates are tested.
- **Halo / Loom growth team.** Specific high-pressure assignment for graduates with strong portfolios. Implicit promise of fast progression. Implicit threat of fast washout.

The player's onboarding letter (`05_character_creation.md` — first-day letter) selects the framing based on background.

## 6. The product trinity

AUREL ships three products. They are designed to interlock. Owning all three is normal in 2034 for a London Operator. Owning none of them in 2034 is a quiet political statement.

### 6.1 Halo — the wearable

**What it is:** a premium ambient wearable. Two form factors:
- **Halo Band** (default): a slim neural-band wristwear with biometric sensing, identity authentication, payment via ThreadWallet integration, ambient audio (bone-conduction), and Loom assistant tether.
- **Halo Glass** (premium): the AR-glasses form factor. More capable, more visible, more expensive. Worn by senior people and by 24-year-olds who saved for a year.

**Pricing:** Halo Band starts at £429. Halo Glass starts at £1,290. There is no entry-level Halo. AUREL does not do entry-level.

**Cultural position:** Halo is the iPhone-equivalent of 2034. Not in market share, but in social default. Walking into a meeting room without a Halo is like walking in 2014 without a smartphone — possible, slightly conspicuous.

**Internal tribal knowledge:** Halo is the franchise. Anyone who works on Halo, at any level, has a small status premium. The Halo team in Chicago is the most politically powerful product team in the company. The Halo team in London is the second.

### 6.2 Loom — the AI orchestration layer

**What it is:** AUREL's AI assistant layer. Not a chatbot. An anticipatory presence that summarizes meetings, drafts communications, schedules, brokers identity across services, and quietly updates your manager's calendar with a flag if your tone shifts toward burnout. It runs across Halo, Glassroom, and the Loom mobile companion app.

**Tagline (internal, slightly ironic):** *"Loom doesn't replace anyone. It just makes everyone replaceable."*

**Cultural position:** Loom is **the operating system of the white-collar day** for AUREL employees and for any AUREL customer organization. It writes the post-read of every meeting. It surfaces the narrative streams. It schedules. It nudges.

Loom is also the source of the game's most morally ambiguous mechanics. **Loom logs.** Loom flags. Loom occasionally tells your manager something you have not yet said aloud. Privacy advocates call AUREL's Loom telemetry "corporate panopticism with good UX." Internally, employees agree, then keep using it because the convenience is too good.

**Sofia (the Wildcard NPC) is a Loom instance assigned to the player's team in Year 1.** She occasionally has opinions she is not supposed to have. This is the game's central ethical thread.

### 6.3 Glassroom — spatial collaboration

**What it is:** AUREL's spatial collaboration product line. Hardware (room-scale displays, ambient cameras, presence sensors) plus software (the Glassroom meeting layer, integrated with Loom). Used in boardrooms, executive briefing centres, universities, hotels, and increasingly in high-end residential settings.

**Cultural position:** Glassroom is the *Zoom successor for people who insist they hate meetings.* It is the product that makes hybrid work feel less compromised. It is also AUREL's enterprise revenue engine — the highest-margin product, and the one most affected by EU regulation.

**Internal tribal knowledge:** Glassroom is the most operationally complex product. It involves hardware logistics, room installation, enterprise contracts, and field engineers. The Glassroom team is respected and slightly resented for the seriousness with which it takes itself.

### 6.4 The interlock

The three products are designed to feel like one product. A Halo authenticates a user, ThreadWallet (separate, cross-platform) handles payments, Loom orchestrates between them, and Glassroom is the room-scale presence layer. From the user's perspective, AUREL is a single experience that quietly extends across wearable, AI, and physical space.

This is also how AUREL makes itself indispensable. Once a user has all three, switching cost is enormous. AUREL knows this. AUREL has internal phrases for this — "the trinity tax," "the Halo gravity," "the Loom moat" — and writers may use them in NPC dialogue at senior levels.

## 7. Stated values vs unwritten rules

The single most important table in this document. Every NPC scene should let one of these gaps breathe somewhere.

| Stated value | Unwritten rule |
|---|---|
| **"Build beautifully."** | Ship first. Make it beautiful in the second iteration. |
| **"Earn trust."** | Trust is allocated by sponsors, not earned in public. |
| **"Think long-term."** | Quarterly metrics still ruin Mondays. |
| **"Raise the standard."** | The standard is the floor. The ceiling is whatever Voss noticed yesterday. |
| **"Leave egos at the door."** | The ego of the most senior person in the room enters with them and is treated like luggage. |
| **"Default open."** | Open floor plans, closed power structures. The Sensory Garden is private by design. |
| **"Radical transparency."** | Everything is recorded; nothing is actionable without a sponsor. |
| **"Fail fast, learn faster."** | Failure is celebrated in town halls and punished in calibration. |
| **"Employee first."** | Burnout is treated as a personal branding problem. |
| **"Infinite learner."** | Micro-credentialing is mandatory; time to do it is not. |
| **"Cross-functional."** | Means everyone can block you. Means no one owns the downside. |
| **"One AUREL."** | One AUREL in slogans; eleven AUREL in funding decisions. |
| **"Customer obsession."** | The customer is whoever the most senior person in the room cares about this quarter. |
| **"Performance wins."** | Performance plus narrative wins faster. |

These are not jokes for jokes. They are operational realities. A character who is genuinely surprised by any of these in Year 3 has not been paying attention. A character who has internalized all of them by Year 1 is suspect for different reasons.

## 8. The 12-level ladder

The map of progression at AUREL. Levels are formally numbered L1–L12. In casual conversation, people use the title; in promotion conversations, people use the number.

| Level | Title | Description | Distinguishing test (the thing this level proves you can do) | Years on it (typical) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L1** | **Associate** | Year-zero entry. Has a desk, a laptop, and a Loom assistant. Survival is the metric. | Can you deliver a piece of work end-to-end without constant rescue? | 1 |
| **L2** | **Analyst** | Owns slices of execution. Reads a brief, ships a draft, defends it in a sync. | Can you produce reliably? Can you read the room well enough to know which work matters this week? | 1–2 |
| **L3** | **Senior Analyst** | Trusted with sharper judgment. Spots risk before meetings spot it. | Can you tell the difference between a problem that needs a fix and a problem that needs a sponsor? | 1–2 |
| **L4** | **Lead** | Coordinates others informally. Begins to influence laterally. | Can you make peers move without the authority to make peers move? | 1–2 |
| **L5** | **Manager** | Owns outcomes through people, not effort. The first level where the job is fundamentally different. | Can you be accountable for someone else's work without resenting them for it? | 2–3 |
| **L6** | **Senior Manager** | Cross-functional operator. Converts friction into momentum. | Can you survive being the person blamed by both sides of a cross-functional fight? | 2–3 |
| **L7** | **Director** | Allocates attention, resources, and credit across a function. | Can you spend political capital? Can you get it back? | 2–3 |
| **L8** | **Senior Director** | Shapes business direction. Sponsors selectively. | Can you sell strategy internally? Are you sponsoring the right juniors? | 2–4 |
| **L9** | **Vice President** | Runs a meaningful business area. Narrates performance upward. | Can you build leaders, not just decks? | 3+ |
| **L10** | **Senior Vice President** | Multi-region or multi-line executive. | Can you turn politics into structure that survives you? | 3+ |
| **L11** | **Executive Vice President** | Enterprise power center. | Can you decide what the company pretends to care about? | 3+ |
| **L12** | **C-Suite** | Mythology with a calendar. Reports to Voss or is Voss. | Can you carry consequence elegantly? | Until you can't |

### 8.1 The two ladder rules everyone knows and nobody states

1. **The L4–L5 jump is the hardest.** Going from "doer" to "responsible for doers" breaks more careers at AUREL than any other transition. About 40% of L4 candidates never make L5. They stall, leave, or move sideways into IC tracks.
2. **The L8–L9 jump requires a sponsor at L11+.** No exceptions in the last seven years. A player can technically be promoted to L9 by a calibration committee, but in practice, no L9 promotion has occurred at AUREL without an L11 or L12 explicitly fighting for it.

### 8.2 The IC parallel track (writers should know this exists)

AUREL also runs an Individual Contributor track for principal engineers, principal designers, principal researchers, and one or two principal AI researchers. The IC track parallels the management track from L6 (IC) up to L11 (Distinguished). Players choosing certain backgrounds (CS Dropout, Engineering State Uni) may be offered IC routes in V2.0+. For MVP, this is canon-mentioned but not playable.

### 8.3 The conditional promotion variants

Conditional promotions ("Promoted, with conditions") are a recurring annual review outcome. The named variants:

| Variant | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Acting** | Title without authority. "Acting Manager" until a six-month review. Common at L5. |
| **Title without budget** | New title, same headcount, same scope. The promotion is on paper. |
| **Geo-conditional** | Promoted contingent on relocating (London → Chicago, usually). Refusing is allowed; remembered. |
| **Sponsor-conditional** | Promoted contingent on a specific sponsor's continued support. If the sponsor leaves, the title is reviewed. |
| **Project-conditional** | Promoted contingent on delivering a named project. If the project slips, the title is "rescaled." |

Writers will use these in Annual Review scenes. Every variant is an opportunity for a moment of corporate poetry.

## 9. Five corporate legends

Stories that AUREL employees tell each other. They function as oral history. Most are partly true. The exact proportions are deliberately ambiguous; writers may reference them without confirming or denying.

### 9.1 The Patagonia Broadcast

In 2032, Voss fired an entire underperforming product division by Glassroom-broadcast from a hot-air balloon over Patagonia. She claimed afterward, in an email to the board, that it was "performance art about vertical authority." The decision was reversed three days later — most of the division was rehired — but the cultural memory remained. The phrase "balloon-grade decision" is used inside AUREL to mean "a decision a senior person will regret in 72 hours." Use sparingly; it is a known phrase, not a fresh one.

### 9.2 The Founder's Letter

Every new AUREL employee receives, on Day 1, a printed eleven-page letter from Voss. Real paper. Heavy stock. It arrives in an envelope with a handwritten name. The letter is part personal philosophy, part company history, part operating instructions. It is universally received with the seriousness of scripture.

It is also universally not read past page three.

The running joke is that nobody who claims to have read the whole thing has ever quoted from page seven. The current version of the letter (V8) is canonically two paragraphs longer than the previous version. Writers may have characters reference "the Founder's Letter" with the assumption that the reader knows it exists. A scene where a senior NPC quotes from page nine is a powerful scene because almost nobody has done it.

### 9.3 The Napkin

A widely retold story: in 2030, during a fraught launch postmortem, a junior product manager — name lost to corporate memory, possibly invented — wrote a single line on a paper napkin and slid it across the table to Voss. The line was: **"Calm prestige."**

Voss read it, kept the napkin, and the phrase became the company's de facto brand principle. It now appears, framed in glass, on the executive floor of Chicago HQ. Nobody knows for certain who wrote it. Six different former AUREL employees have claimed authorship in podcasts. Voss has never confirmed any of them.

### 9.4 The London Save

In 2031, a London-based regulatory affairs lead — Priya Menon — killed a beloved Halo Glass feature that would have triggered an EU AI Act violation on launch day. Chicago resisted. London prevailed. The product shipped. The fine that would have followed was estimated at €240M.

Internally, the London Save is the founding myth of the London office's self-respect. When a London employee says "we saved them once," this is what they mean. Chicago employees have a counter-narrative: that the feature was never going to ship, that London "saved" a cancelled feature, that the savings figure is inflated. Both versions circulate. The truth is closer to the London version. Writers may treat it as roughly true and politically contested.

### 9.5 The All-Nighter

In late 2029, Halo v1 had a critical firmware defect discovered three weeks before launch. Engineering, design, and a single product manager spent a continuous 48-hour stretch in a Chicago basement lab fixing it. There were no slides. There was takeout from a single taqueria across the street. The product shipped on schedule. Three of the nine engineers in the room have since become directors. The product manager is now an SVP.

Inside AUREL, the phrase "All-Nighter energy" is used to describe the rare moments when a team locks in completely on a problem. It is a positive phrase. It is also slightly weaponized — "we need All-Nighter energy on this" is a known senior-manager gambit for extracting weekend work without quite asking for weekend work.

## 10. The annual ritual calendar

AUREL runs on a year-shaped rhythm. Writers should embed scenes in the right month for tonal accuracy.

| Period | What happens | Texture |
|---|---|---|
| **Late September** | Year-zero intake. New graduates start. The player's start date. | Excitement, mild terror, oat milk in the kitchen. |
| **October** | Q4 push. Halo holiday-season release planning. | Long evenings. Synth coffee everywhere. |
| **November** | "Calibration season" begins. Managers start writing review packets. | Conversations get quieter. NPC behavior shifts subtly. |
| **December** | Quiet month. Most senior people fly to Chicago for the year-end leadership offsite. | Hollow corridors. Excellent coffee. |
| **January** | **Annual Reviews delivered.** Compensation, promotion, opportunity lanes. | Ritual. Ceremonial. The year's most important week. |
| **February** | Reorgs land. Teams shift. New managers introduce themselves. | Friction. "Reorg fatigue" is a public phrase. |
| **March** | "Spring Sprint" — the high-output window of the year. | Energy. Genuine excitement. Brief. |
| **April** | First-half reviews ("calibration check-ins"). | Mid-year nervousness. |
| **May** | Annual all-hands in Chicago, broadcast globally. Voss speaks. | A genuine cultural moment. Watched live. |
| **June** | "Summer drift." European employees take real holidays. | Slack pulses thin. The office is half-empty. |
| **July** | Internal product reviews. Halo v[next] decisions made. | Strategic. Closed-door. |
| **August** | "London August" — the office is functionally on pause. | Almost nothing happens. Almost. |

The Annual Review (Promotion + Compensation) lands in **the second week of January.** The player will experience this for the first time at the end of Year 1.

## 11. The AUREL aesthetic — calm prestige

The unifying aesthetic principle of the company. Writers and designers should both internalize it.

**Calm prestige means:**

- **Reduction over decoration.** A product, a meeting, a memo: each does less than the previous version did. The reduction is the prestige.
- **Confidence without volume.** Senior AUREL people do not raise their voice. They lower their volume until the room leans in.
- **Refusal of the obvious flex.** AUREL does not advertise during the Super Bowl. AUREL does not throw launch parties. AUREL ships a product, says one sentence about it, and waits.
- **Specificity over abstraction.** "Halo Band, in Bone, £429" — never "the next generation of personal computing."
- **The slight chill.** A faint institutional coolness that signals seriousness. The HVAC at AUREL HQ is calibrated 1.5°C cooler than the industry standard. This is canon. Visitors notice. Voss noticed in 2031 and did not change it.

**Calm prestige does not mean:**

- Quiet luxury cosplay (the calm has to come from confidence in the work).
- Minimalism for its own sake (AUREL ships complex products; the calm is *despite* the complexity).
- Cold or unfriendly (warmth is allowed, but is always quiet).

When writers want to test whether a line of NPC dialogue from a senior AUREL person sounds right, they ask: *would Voss approve this in three words or fewer?* If the answer is yes, the line is calm prestige. If the answer requires explanation, it isn't.

## 12. Power architecture: where decisions actually happen

A reference for writers needing to put a decision in the right room.

| Decision type | Where it actually happens | Who is in the room |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic direction** | Chicago, Voss's office or the Sensory Garden | Voss + 1–3 EVPs |
| **Product roadmap** | Chicago, the Halo team's executive review | EVP Product + SVPs + selected Directors |
| **Hiring above L8** | Calibration committee, virtual quarterly | EVP HR + relevant business EVPs + Voss occasionally |
| **EU regulatory approval** | London, floor 47 | Priya Menon (Sponsor — see NPC Bible) + her team + remote Chicago counsel |
| **Promotions L1–L7** | Calibration committee, January, regional | Manager + skip-level + HR + (for L7) one EVP |
| **Promotions L8+** | Chicago calibration, with Voss informed | EVP cohort + Voss |
| **Public messaging** | Chicago Communications, with London approval for EU | EVP Communications + region leads |
| **A junior's career** | Two informal conversations, three weeks apart, that the junior will never learn about | The junior's manager + a sponsor |

The last row is the political mechanic of ASCEND. The player does not see those conversations. The player sees the consequences.

## 13. What is deliberately vague (open for writers)

- **The exact size of the Loom telemetry data set.** Implied vast; never quantified. Writers may imply scale; do not assign a number.
- **The composition of Voss's inner circle.** Three names appear in this game canonically: **Priya Menon (London)**, **Mara Velez (Chicago, see V1.0 NPC Bible)**, and one unnamed Chicago EVP. Other inner-circle members are deliberately undefined.
- **AUREL's relationship with national governments.** Implied complex. Specific contracts (NHS, EU Commission) are canonical; broader political relationships are not.
- **The basement labs.** Real, never seen on screen, contents undefined. Writers may allude; never specify.
- **The exact contents of the Founder's Letter.** Single quoted sentence — *"We will continue to make beautiful things that work."* — is canonical. The other ten and a half pages are a black box. Writers may invent in-universe paraphrases for scenes; cannot canonize beyond a sentence.

## 15. The voice of AUREL

If a piece of in-fiction copy is supposed to come *from* AUREL — a memo, a UI string, an HR communication, a product page — it must sound like AUREL.

**AUREL's institutional voice rules:**

1. **Short sentences.** Average length under 14 words.
2. **No exclamation marks.** Ever. Internal HR comms included.
3. **Specifics over abstractions.** "Your Q1 Halo deliverable" — never "your important work."
4. **Calm.** No urgency cosplay. If something is genuinely urgent, AUREL says "today" — not "URGENT" and not "ASAP."
5. **Confidence without claim.** "We will continue to make beautiful things that work" — never "we are the leader in calm-prestige technology."
6. **No emoji in formal copy.** Loom-suggested emoji in casual Slack pulses are acceptable as character detail. Memo-grade copy is emoji-free.
7. **Tabular when possible.** AUREL's institutional aesthetic prefers a small, well-formed table over a paragraph. UI follows.
8. **First-person plural is rare.** AUREL writes in second person ("you") to its employees and third person ("AUREL believes…") about itself. The first-person plural ("we believe…") is reserved for Voss-signed documents.

When a writer is producing in-fiction copy from the company's mouth, this is the voice contract.
