# 01 — World Bible: 2034

**Owner:** Narrative Lead
**Audience:** Anyone writing a question, an NPC line, a UI label, or a piece of in-fiction copy.
**Status:** Canon. Anything that contradicts this document is wrong unless this document is wrong, in which case it gets updated.
**Scope of this document:** the world. The people in it. The shape of work. **Not** AUREL. AUREL has its own bible (`02_company_bible.md`).

---

## 1. How to use this document

This is a reference, not a story. Writers consult it the way an architect consults a building code: not to be inspired by it, but to make sure what they build doesn't violate it.

Three rules:

1. **Canon binds; it does not narrate.** Facts in this document are settled. Writers may reveal them sideways, allude to them, or never mention them. They may not contradict them.
2. **Specifics belong here, not in scenes.** When a scene needs a detail (the average rent in Zone 3, the name of the AR meeting tool, the year ThreadWallet became mainstream), it comes from this document. Writers do not invent. If a needed fact does not exist here, propose it via a doc PR; once merged, it is canon for everyone.
3. **The world is observed, not explained.** No NPC ever delivers a paragraph of exposition about 2034. The reader assembles 2034 from accumulated specifics. This document equips writers with those specifics.

If a writer has read this once, they should be able to answer: *what does Tuesday morning feel like for a 21-year-old at AUREL London in 2034?* If the answer is not vivid, the writer should re-read.

---

## 2. The premise of 2034 in three sentences

AI did not end work. It compressed junior work, automated the polite drudgery that used to justify large entry teams, and made human judgment, taste, and political navigation more valuable than the people selling automation promised. The visible result is a world where everyone smiles in 4K and quietly wonders if they are being made obsolete by software, or by someone younger who uses it better.

That is the emotional weather of ASCEND. Not apocalypse. Not utopia. Something more irritating than either.

## 3. Timeline 2024–2034

A year-by-year spine. Each entry is canon. Use it to date references, anchor NPC backstories, and decide what counts as "remembered" versus "always was."

### 2024
The year ChatGPT becomes a verb at work. Hybrid is normalized but contested. Most companies have a "three days in office" policy that nobody enforces. Junior consultants discover that the first draft of a deck can be done in fifteen minutes; senior consultants discover this six months later.

### 2025
The first wave of AI-driven layoffs in finance, law, and marketing — small numbers, big headlines. Anthropic's labor-market report becomes the document everyone cites and nobody reads. Major banks cut graduate intake by ~25%. The phrase "AI-resistant skills" enters HR vocabulary and immediately becomes meaningless.

### 2026
Big Tech reduces graduate intake by another 30%. Universities begin emergency talks about post-graduate employment pipelines. The "year-zero entry" pilot programs launch — raw graduates thrown into the deep end with AI tools and an expectation of immediate output. The first "AI orchestrator" job titles appear, mostly as relabelled junior roles.

### 2027
**Credential collapse begins.** AI fully absorbs first-pass research, drafting, scheduling, and basic analysis. Universities pivot toward "competency stacks" — bundles of micro-credentials verified by blockchain ledgers. Traditional degrees do not die; they get demoted from sufficient to necessary, and from necessary to occasionally helpful.

### 2028
First major class-action lawsuits against AI-driven layoffs in the EU. The legal outcomes are mixed; the cultural outcome is decisive — public sentiment turns against companies that lay off humans to deploy AI. Big Tech learns to redeploy quietly.

### 2029
NHS signs the first major government contract for digital identity infrastructure. This is the year the wearable identity platforms (subdermal NFC, neural-band authentication) move from luxury to plausible. Blockchain-verified credential ledgers go mainstream in hiring. The phrase "show me the verified stack" replaces "where did you go to school" in serious recruiting conversations.

### 2030
**The EU AI Act, second wave.** Brussels publishes the "Architect Layer" rules — a regulatory framework that distinguishes AI used to *decide* about humans (heavily restricted) from AI used to *assist* humans (lightly regulated). Most companies find ways to call everything assistance.

Persistent AI assistants become standard in white-collar work. They sit in meetings, summarize, and quietly nudge. Productivity is up. So is ambient anxiety.

### 2031
The first Big Tech antitrust split. The result is not what regulators wanted: instead of weakening platform power, the breakup creates "platform ecosystems" — multiple companies that look independent but settle into a tight federation. Concentration shifted addresses; it didn't lose mass.

### 2032
**London becomes the regulatory translator of Europe.** Companies base their European HQs in London not because the UK government is friendly (it isn't, particularly) but because London talent can read the EU AI Act faster than anyone else and convert it into product strategy. Singapore plays the same role for APAC. Austin remains the second city of US tech but gives up on becoming the first. Mumbai and São Paulo emerge as the consumer-market hubs nobody can ignore.

### 2033
AR contact lenses begin to replace AR glasses for early adopters. They are still expensive, still slightly unreliable, still a status object. The cultural transition is not "everyone has them" — it is "you start to notice the people who don't."

### 2034
**Game start.** Hybrid is stable at roughly 60/40 (office days vs. remote days). The labor market has settled into three durable tiers (see §4). The "year-zero" hiring model is normal. Sponsorship has replaced manager goodwill as the only reliable promotion mechanism. Mental health is discussed openly and treated as an individual optimization problem, not a structural one. Ambition is fashionable again, but must be worn lightly.

The player joins AUREL London on a Monday in late September.

## 4. Macro-economic landscape

### 4.1 The three-tier labor market

By 2034, the white-collar workforce sorts into three durable tiers. The boundaries are not legal; they are observable. Everyone at AUREL knows which tier they are in. Almost nobody says it aloud.

| Tier | Description | Typical roles | What they get |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Architects** | Senior strategists, creatives, human-relation roles AI cannot replicate. The ones who decide what AI does. | VPs and above, founders, AI ethics leads, executive coaches, "human alignment specialists." | Sponsorship, equity, narrative control, the room. |
| **Operators** | Workers who manage AI agents, verify outputs, handle exceptions, and translate ambiguity into action. The bulk of the white-collar economy. | Product managers, AI orchestrators, mid-level engineers, regulated-domain specialists. | Stable income, mortgage-grade salaries, ambient insecurity. |
| **The Drift** | A floating population of gig workers without firm attachment. Often highly skilled. Often choosing this. Often not. | Freelance designers, translators-without-borders, weekend coders, "fractional" everything. | Flexibility, no benefits, a five-tab calendar. |

Movement between tiers is possible but rare and asymmetric. Architects almost never become Operators voluntarily. Operators move to The Drift through layoff or burnout, and back through luck. Drift workers occasionally rejoin the Operator tier; almost none reach Architect.

The player begins as an Operator, with the explicit expectation of becoming an Architect. The game is whether they can.

### 4.2 What happened to graduate hiring

The phrase "graduate scheme" is now a mild anachronism, like "trainee" or "junior associate" before it. Companies still hire raw graduates. They just stopped pretending the relationship is paternal.

The replacement model is **year-zero entry.** A new graduate joins, is given a laptop and a stack of AI tools, and is expected to produce within four weeks. There is no rotation, no nurture period, no "spend your first year learning the business." There is the desk and the work and the assumption that you will figure out how to combine them.

The compression is brutal but legible. People who survive year zero come out of it with skills senior peers genuinely envy. People who do not survive it leave at month seven and do something else. The dropout rate is not advertised.

### 4.3 Micro-credentials and the credential stack

The visible CV in 2034 is a "stack." It looks like this:

```
Cambridge MEng (2031)
+ Anthropic Applied Alignment Cert (2032)
+ AUREL Loom Orchestration Level 2 (2033)
+ EU AI Act Compliance Practicum (2033)
+ Verified portfolio: 6 deployed projects, 11k weighted reviews
```

Degrees are still helpful. They are no longer sufficient. A 2026 PPE from LSE without a credential stack signals "did not adapt." A community college background plus a strong stack plus three deployed projects signals "delivered."

This shifts the recruiting power center. The recruiter who matters in 2034 is the one who can read a credential stack and tell which signals are real, which are inflated, and which are decorative. AUREL has a small team of these recruiters and they are very, very well paid.

### 4.4 Geographic concentration

The global tech ring of 2034 has five cities of meaningful weight:

- **San Francisco / Silicon Valley** — still dominant, no longer mythological. The mythology has migrated.
- **Austin** — the second US city. Power-second. Talent factory. "Where you go to do the work nobody back home will see."
- **London** — Europe's regulatory translator. Where strategy is converted into something Brussels can stomach.
- **Singapore** — the same role for APAC. Cleaner, faster, more obedient to its government, harder to live in if you have opinions.
- **Mumbai / São Paulo** — the consumer markets nobody can ignore. Where revenue is decided, not where strategy is written.

Tokyo, Berlin, Paris, Toronto, Tel Aviv, and Bangalore matter. They do not anchor. The five-city ring decides what counts as "global."

### 4.5 Cost of living, briefly

For a 23-year-old graduate in 2034 London on £45,000 base, the rent in Zone 2 is £1,650/month for a room in a flat-share. Council tax is £140. Pret a Manger Subscription Plus is £42/month and is worth it. A pint in Shoreditch is £8.40. The Tube is contactless via ThreadWallet without thinking. The maths does not work and everyone knows it. The maths working is one of the unspoken Architect privileges.

This is canon. Use it when grounding scenes. Do not write a graduate "comfortably affording" a London flat. They cannot.

## 5. Technology culture in 2034

### 5.1 Communication stack

Email is not dead. It is demoted. Email survives for legal documentation, HR formalities, government communications, and people over fifty. Almost nobody under thirty-five uses it for daily coordination.

The dominant communication stack at a 2034 white-collar company looks like this:

| Channel | Used for | Status signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Slack pulses** | Ambient team coordination. Pulses are short, tagged, AI-summarized into "narrative streams" so nobody has to scroll. | Default. Everyone has them. |
| **Narrative streams** | Persistent, threaded, AI-summarized work feeds. The "official version of what happened this week." | Reading them is operator-grade. Writing them well is architect-grade. |
| **Async voice notes** | Cross-time-zone alignment. Three-minute clips replacing the "quick call." | Senior people send them; junior people transcribe them. |
| **AR meetings** | Holographic presence in hybrid pods. The default for synchronous meetings with anyone not in the room. | Everyone uses them. Nobody admits they are exhausting. |
| **Decision rooms** | A specific meeting type — twenty minutes, no screens, one decision, recorded summary auto-generated. | Architect-grade meeting hygiene. Calling one signals authority. |
| **Email** | Legal, HR, government. Sometimes used for theatrical formality. | "I'll put it in writing" is now a threat. |

### 5.2 What "a meeting" means

A 2034 meeting is a **layered object**, not a slot in a calendar. It typically has three parts:

1. **The pre-read.** An AI-assembled brief that summarizes context, prior decisions, and open questions. Reading it is mandatory; nobody admits to skipping it; everyone has skipped it.
2. **The live window.** Either fifteen minutes (a "sync") or ninety minutes (a "deep session"). Anything between is suspicious.
3. **The post-read.** An AI-generated summary that becomes the "official version of what was said." Selectively quoted later. Weaponized at promotion time.

The elite skill is not talking in the meeting. It is **controlling what the post-read says.** Senior people learn to phrase their points so the summarizer captures them cleanly. Very senior people learn to make the post-read disagree with what was actually said, in their favor, without anyone noticing.

This is a recurring source of dramatic material in ASCEND. Players will encounter the post-read weaponization mechanic in Year 1.

### 5.3 Devices and wearables

The 2034 personal device stack centers on three categories.

- **Phones** still exist. They are smaller, more ambient, and increasingly secondary. The phone is a fallback, not a primary interface.
- **Wearables** are primary. The major categories are:
  - **AR contact lenses** (AuraLens by NXGEN/AUREL competitors; Halo by AUREL has a glasses-form variant for people who don't want lenses). Information overlays. Not yet universal — about 35% of UK urban professionals use them daily.
  - **Subdermal NFC** (ThreadWallet — payment, identity, transit, building access). About 28% adoption; opting out is "technically possible, socially crippling," like refusing a smartphone in 2014.
  - **Neural-band wristwear** — biometric, light cognitive monitoring, AI assistant tether. The most common wearable; ~70% adoption.
- **AI companions** (EchoOrbs, Loom voice agents, others) — anticipatory presences that summarize, draft, suggest, and occasionally judge. Most professionals use one constantly. Everyone has a story about the time their AI flagged something to their manager that they had not yet said aloud.

### 5.4 Status of tech roles

The hierarchy of cool inside a 2034 tech company is not what 2024 expected.

| Role family | 2024 status | 2034 status |
|---|---|---|
| **AI trainers / human-alignment specialists** | Niche, weird | Rock stars |
| **Product managers (orchestrators)** | Mid | High — they decide what AI does |
| **Product managers (Jira janitors)** | High-ish | Endangered |
| **Software engineers** | Fetishised | Respected, not worshipped |
| **AI ethics / regulatory leads** | "Compliance" | Power center |
| **Designers** | Underrated | Slightly less underrated |
| **Sales** | Quietly powerful | Loudly powerful |
| **Data scientists** | Hot | Operator-grade |
| **HR** | Invisible | Visible — and increasingly suspected |

Two specific 2034 dynamics writers should track:

- **"Orchestrator" PMs vs. "Jira janitor" PMs.** Both have the same title. They are different jobs. Orchestrators decide what AI builds. Jira janitors write tickets. The first is on track for VP. The second is on track for replacement. Nobody admits the distinction in public; everyone navigates it in private.
- **The rise of the "human alignment specialist."** This role barely existed in 2024. By 2034 it is one of the most senior individual contributor tracks at any major AI company — the person who decides how AI behaves in edge cases. They are paid like principal engineers and treated like principal engineers and have approximately none of the engineering background. This causes friction. The friction is ours to write.

## 6. Big Tech in 2034

### 6.1 Power and regulation

Big Tech in 2034 is more regulated, and still more powerful, than it was in 2024. The EU has learned to fine, audit, and occasionally embarrass them. Consumers and institutions remained too dependent on the ecosystems to detach. The state's leverage is real but partial; the dependence is total.

The 2031 antitrust split fragmented some giants but produced "platform ecosystems" — federations of nominally independent companies that interoperate so smoothly the user cannot tell them apart. From a regulator's perspective, concentration changed addresses. From a user's perspective, nothing changed except the UI.

### 6.2 Cultural dominance

The cultural footprint of a major tech company in 2034 is closest to **Hollywood in the 1950s.** Everyone consumes their products. Everyone mocks their executives. Their internal politics leak constantly, are discussed widely, and influence cultural language well beyond their direct customer base. A Big Tech CEO is recognizable to a 19-year-old in Lagos. Their letter to shareholders gets group-chatted. Their bad meeting becomes a meme.

Inside the company, this means employees feel like they are working at a place that *matters culturally* in a way that almost no other industry matches. This is the fuel of ambition. It is also the source of the surveillance fatigue.

### 6.3 What it feels like to work at one

Working at a Big Tech behemoth in 2034 feels, in approximate order:

1. **Like joining a nation-state with a gym membership.** The perks are generous. The mission is vague enough to project anything onto.
2. **Curiously medieval on the inside.** There are fiefdoms, patronage networks, symbols, inherited grudges, and internal dialects. The only modern part is the furniture.
3. **Surveillance is ambient.** Your AI assistant logs you. Your manager's AI assistant logs you. The narrative stream has a record. Nobody discusses this. Everyone behaves as if discussed.
4. **The mission is real.** The cynicism is also real. Both at once.
5. **The food is excellent.** This is widely understood to be a retention strategy.
