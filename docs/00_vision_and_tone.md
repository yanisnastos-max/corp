# 00 — Vision & Tone Style Guide

**Owner:** Narrative Lead
**Audience:** Every writer, designer, and developer who touches text in this project. Yes, including UI labels and error messages.
**Status:** Foundational. If a piece of writing in ASCEND fails this guide, it gets rewritten — regardless of who wrote it.

---

## 1. Why this document exists

ASCEND is a text game. Words are the rendering engine. There is no art, no soundtrack to hide behind, no animation flourish that masks a flat sentence. If the text is wrong, the game is wrong.

This guide is the voice contract. It exists so that:
- Five different writers can produce 120 questions without the seams showing.
- Reviewers can reject prose with a specific reason, not "this feels off."
- The voice survives translation, expansion, and three years of contributors.

Read this once. Re-read the relevant section before writing. Keep it open while editing.

---

## 2. The voice in one paragraph

ASCEND sounds like an unusually observant friend who has spent a decade in a tech company, kept a journal nobody was supposed to read, and figured out that the best way to describe a corporation is to describe it accurately. Dry, sharp, observational. Funny because it is true. Cynical about systems, not nihilistic about people. Positive about growth, suspicious of slogans. The voice does not explain its jokes. The voice does not soften its diagnoses. The voice respects the reader enough to assume they have already noticed what is being pointed at.

If a sentence in ASCEND could appear unchanged on the back of a Pret a Manger sandwich box, rewrite it.

---

## 3. The Galloway/Godin Test

Every scenario, option, and review line passes through this two-question gate before it ships.

**Question 1 (Galloway):** Would Scott Galloway find this funny?
- Galloway funny means: blunt, market-savvy, slightly cynical, uses metaphor and pop culture, reveals a power dynamic in twelve words or fewer.
- Galloway not funny means: a pun without a point, a corporate joke that has been told before, a punchline that punches down at people without power.

**Question 2 (Godin):** Would Seth Godin find this true?
- Godin true means: it identifies the gap between what people say and what they do, it generalizes to a real principle, it is short enough to remember.
- Godin not true means: it is true *only* inside the game, it is decorative, it is a feeling instead of an observation.

**Both must be yes.** A line that is funny but not true is a tweet. A line that is true but not funny is a memo. ASCEND is neither.

If a line passes only one, mark it `[GG-fail]` in the draft and rewrite before merge.

---

## 4. The Smirk Test

After Galloway/Godin, one more gate: read the line aloud as if a 28-year-old product manager has just heard it during a budget meeting. Does she smirk?

- A nod is not enough. A nod means "I understand."
- A laugh is too much. A laugh means we tried too hard.
- A smirk is the goal. A smirk means "I have lived this and you know it."

If the line gets a polite half-smile or nothing, the line is not done.

---

## 5. Tone non-negotiables

These are not preferences. These are rules.

1. **No saccharine.** No "you've got this," no "the only way to fail is to give up," no character growth montages set to inspirational quotes. Growth in ASCEND is real. It is also expensive and inconvenient.
2. **No cynicism for its own sake.** We are not edgelords. Cynical-funny means revealing a system; cynical-empty means complaining at the camera.
3. **No TED Talk energy.** No three-act inspirational stories with a tidy lesson. The world does not have tidy lessons. Neither does this game.
4. **No corporate-speak in the narrator voice.** The narrator never says "leverage," "synergy," "circle back," "value-add," "north star," or "deep dive" without quotation marks. NPCs may say these things — that is characterization, not voice.
5. **No explanation of the joke.** If you find yourself writing a sentence that explains the previous sentence, delete one of them.
6. **No "right answer" telegraphing.** Options must read as if a smart adult could plausibly pick any of them. The game does not telegraph virtue.
7. **No 13-year-old talk-down.** The audience starts at 13. Treat them as the smartest 13-year-old you have ever met. They have already noticed the irony. Don't underline it.

## 6. Sentence shapes we use

ASCEND has a rhythm. Writers should internalize it. The following shapes recur and form the texture of the prose.

- **The setup-then-detonate sentence.** A neutral observation, comma, a sharp specific. *"The coffee is excellent. This is widely understood to be a retention strategy."*
- **The slightly-too-long descriptor.** Three modifiers where two would do, used to make the absurdity audible. *"A polished, well-rested, faintly menacing director enters the room."*
- **The corporate noun-phrase used straight.** No irony quotes, no distance. *"The stairwell smells of oat milk and anxiety."* The reader supplies the irony.
- **The aphoristic close.** A scene ends on a sentence that could be a Post-it. *"Promotion now would be a bug, not a feature."*
- **The diagnosed metaphor.** Metaphors are allowed but must explain. *"Your calendar is a weapon"* — yes. *"Time is a river"* — no, decorative.
- **The deadpan stage direction.** Action lines are written as if they are mildly disappointed. *"She does not look up when you sit down."*

### Sentence shapes we avoid

- The exclamation. We do not use exclamation marks in narrator prose. NPCs may use them; the narrator does not. This is non-negotiable.
- The rhetorical question to the reader. *"Have you ever wondered…"* — never.
- The em-dash-em-dash run-on used as substitute for a real sentence. One em-dash per paragraph maximum.
- The list of three abstract nouns. *"Ambition, integrity, vision"* is a recruiting poster. We are not a recruiting poster.
- The sentence that explains its own subtext. If the line says "this was awkward," the awkwardness is no longer doing the work.

## 7. Banned phrases (with reasons, so writers know when an exception applies)

The default is: do not use these words or phrases in narrator voice. NPCs may use them when characterization demands it.

| Phrase | Why banned | Use only if |
|---|---|---|
| Synergy | Corporate vapor | NPC is being satirized for using it |
| Circle back | Time-wasting verb | NPC voice |
| Leverage (verb) | Empty action word | NPC voice |
| Pivot | Founder cliché | Quoted in NPC dialogue, with the irony intact |
| Value-add | Reduces meaning to noun | Never |
| North star | Visionary cosplay | Never |
| Deep dive | Passive-aggressive workload signal | NPC voice |
| Game-changer | Pre-2018 buzzword | Never |
| Disruptive | Used by everything; means nothing | Never |
| At the end of the day | Filler | Never |
| Move the needle | Quantitative cosplay | Never |
| Best practices | Authority-borrowing phrase | Never |
| Rockstar/ninja/wizard | Embarrassing | Never, including in job titles |
| Hustle | Glorifies overwork | NPC voice, ironic |
| Authentic / authentically | Performative | Never; rephrase |
| Journey (career sense) | Sentimental | Never |
| Passion | The "follow your passion" trap | NPC voice, ironic |
| Family (re: company) | Manipulative; HR-coded | NPC voice, with quotation marks |
| Crushing it / killing it | Aggressive emptiness | NPC voice |
| Touch base | Avoidance language | NPC voice |
| Bandwidth | Person-as-machine metaphor | NPC voice |
| Ping (as verb) | Sometimes acceptable in 2034 setting | Allowed in dialogue; sparingly in narrator |
| Exciting times | Cope phrasing | Never |
| Going forward | Filler | Never |

When a writer needs an exception (because an NPC genuinely says these things), flag the line with `[NPC-tone]` in the draft so review knows it is intentional.

## 8. Tone exemplars — good and bad, side by side

### Setup text

**Bad:**
> Your manager calls you into his office for an exciting discussion about your future. He smiles warmly and tells you that you've been doing great work and he wants to discuss next steps for your career journey at AUREL. You feel a flutter of nervousness — what could it be?

**Why it fails:** "exciting," "smiles warmly," "great work," "career journey," "flutter of nervousness." Five GG-fails in one paragraph. Also explains its own subtext. Also asks the reader a rhetorical question.

**Good:**
> Dana asks if you have a minute. You have noticed that "a minute" inside AUREL is roughly twenty-three. She closes the glass door of the pod with a gentleness that means the conversation will not be casual. Outside, somebody walks past with three coffees and the expression of a person who has lost an argument they did not know they were having.

**Why it works:** Specific (twenty-three minutes, three coffees, glass door). Diagnoses a system (the closed-door tell). Ends on a deadpan stage direction. The reader supplies the irony.

### Option text

**Bad:**
> "I think we should focus on what's best for the team and put aside our personal differences."

**Why it fails:** Saint option. Telegraphs virtue. Could appear unchanged on a poster. No reader will pick this without rolling their eyes.

**Good:**
> "Whatever the room wants. I'll make it work — assuming the room can decide before Friday."

**Why it works:** Compliance with a barb. Reveals the player's read on the room. Specific deadline grounds it. Plausible adult speech.

### Annual review line

**Bad:**
> "Congratulations! Your hard work has paid off and you've earned a promotion. We're thrilled to have you growing with the AUREL family."

**Why it fails:** Exclamations, "hard work has paid off," "thrilled," "AUREL family." Could be a LinkedIn post.

**Good:**
> "Yes. Try not to celebrate in a way that creates evidence."

**Why it works:** Eight words. Diagnoses the entire culture of the building.

## 9. Dialogue rules

NPCs are characterized by *what they say* and *what they refuse to say.* Two writing constraints make this work:

1. **Every named NPC has a distinct verbal habit.** Examples:
   - **Dana Sutcliffe** apologizes for corporate policy as if she invented it. Sentence shape: short, slightly exhausted, occasional self-deprecation.
   - **Priya Menon** uses precise, surgical compliments. No exclamation marks. Ever.
   - **Zara Okafor** speaks fast, slightly performatively, and uses "obviously" too often.
   - **Leo Chen** is deadpan; his jokes are recognizable only to people who are listening.
   - **Sofia (the Vox AI)** uses helpful, then disarmingly philosophical phrasing. She asks questions that have no help-desk answer.
2. **NPCs may use banned phrases.** A manager who says "let's circle back" is being characterized. The narrator must never describe that manager as someone who "wants to circle back" — that puts the narrator inside the cliché.

**Dialogue tags:** prefer `said`. Use other verbs only when they do work. *"Naomi said"* is invisible. *"Naomi observed,"* *"Naomi corrected,"* *"Naomi outlasted the silence"* — each must earn the verb.

**Em-dashes and beats.** A pause inside dialogue should usually be a separate sentence, not a stage direction. *"Promotion." She lets the word sit between you like a legal document. "You are not being promoted."* — yes. *"Promotion," she said, pausing dramatically* — no.

## 10. Specifics over abstractions

Names, numbers, places, and details do more work than adjectives. Always.

- Not "expensive coffee" — "the £6.40 single-origin pour-over."
- Not "a long meeting" — "the ninety-three-minute deep session that ended six minutes after it began."
- Not "the promotion was bittersweet" — "the title arrived; the budget did not."
- Not "she was tired" — "she had answered the same Slack pulse three times this morning."

Specifics ground the world in 2034. They are also the difference between the Galloway/Godin test passing and failing.

## 11. The 2034 register

Light setting touches that should appear regularly without being underlined:

- **Slack pulses** (the evolved, AI-summarized successor to Slack).
- **Narrative streams** (persistent, threaded, AI-summarized work feeds).
- **AR meetings** with holographic participants in hybrid pods.
- **Synth coffee** (cheaper, not better).
- **ThreadWallet** payments (a glance, a tap, a faint subdermal warmth).
- **AuraLens / Halo** wearable glances during conversation that everyone pretends not to notice.
- **Visa clock** language for international students.
- **Compliance** as a noun used the way "ops" used to be used.

These appear in passing. They are never explained. The reader assembles 2034 from context.

## 12. Sensitive topics

ASCEND is rated 13+. The world is sharp; it is not cruel.

- **Mental health** is treated honestly. Burnout, anxiety, and "ambient anxiety" appear regularly. They are not joke material; they are diagnostic material. Lines about mental health pass the Godin test (true) but should not pass the Galloway test (funny) at the cost of the truth.
- **No self-harm content, ever.** Not even in metaphor.
- **Power dynamics involving age, race, gender, sexuality, immigration status, or disability** are observed, never mocked. NPCs who hold those views may exist; the narrator does not endorse them.
- **Money** is talked about specifically and unsentimentally. Salary numbers, rent, equity grants, the gap between gross and net — all in scope. Wealth is not a punchline.
- **Politics-of-the-day** is out of scope. Compliance, regulation, and corporate power are in. We do not write about real elected officials, real countries' policies, or real geopolitical events.

## 13. Quick reference — the writer's checklist

Before submitting any line:

- [ ] Does it pass the Galloway test (funny in a market-savvy, system-revealing way)?
- [ ] Does it pass the Godin test (true about the gap between stated and actual)?
- [ ] Does it pass the Smirk test (a 28-year-old PM smirks, doesn't laugh, doesn't nod)?
- [ ] Are specifics doing the work, not adjectives?
- [ ] Did I avoid banned phrases (or flag intentional `[NPC-tone]` exceptions)?
- [ ] No exclamation marks in narrator voice?
- [ ] No saint option, no idiot option, no joke option that isn't also true?
- [ ] No explained subtext? (If I described the reaction, delete the description and trust the reader.)
- [ ] Does the line still work if a 13-year-old reads it as an adult sentence — i.e., am I respecting them?
- [ ] If this line stood alone on a slide, would I be embarrassed by it?

If any answer is no, the line goes back.

## 14. Voice failure modes — and how to recover

**"This is starting to feel cynical and joyless."**
Recovery: insert a small, true moment of human warmth. Not a hug — a recognition. *Grace remembering your name on day three.* The voice is positive about people; it is the systems that are absurd.

**"This sounds like LinkedIn."**
Recovery: cut every adjective. Then cut every adverb. If a sentence still sounds inspirational, it is.

**"This is too clever — I can hear the writer."**
Recovery: replace one of the two cleverest things in the paragraph with something specific and plain. The reader will reward you.

**"The NPCs all sound the same."**
Recovery: read three of their lines aloud back-to-back. If you can't tell them apart, give one of them a verbal habit (a tic, a refusal, a length preference) and revise.

**"Players keep picking the same option."**
Recovery: rewrite the unchosen options so each one is a plausible adult choice. Often the issue is one option signaling virtue too loudly.
